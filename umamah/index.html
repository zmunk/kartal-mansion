With a history that spans over more than a million years, Palestine has played an important role in human civilization. It is where society as we know it, the alphabet, religion, and literature developed and come together to create the cultures and ideas that we enjoy in our every day life. On your adventure through Palestine, you will enjoy many religious, historical, and archaeological sites. However, Palestine has more to offer than just histoy. There are walks and hikes through beautiful valleys, along coasts, and through desert hills, towns, and ancient marketplaces. The cuisine is nothing short of sumptuous and, most importantly, the warmth brought to you by the Palestinian hospitality will make you feel right at home.

Food


1. Al-Aqsa Sweets

For those with a sweet tooth, Al-Aqsa Sweets is at your service. This place is well known for inventing Palestine's staple dessert dish: Knafah. A stringy, cheesy, sugary desert unlike any other. Al-Aqsa Sweets hero's this staple palestinian dish using semolina, cheese, syrup and nothing else. When visiting Palestine, a trip to the country's cheesy capital, Nablus, is a must!


2. Dar Al-Balad

Dar Al Balad is the place for you if you want an authentic Palestinian experience, and they do have a reputation for high-quality Palestinian food. To get a true taste of Bethlehem, stopping by the Dar Al Balad should be at the top of your list of things to do. Their grand interior is bound to wow you, when you sit down and have a few puffs on their shisha pipes.


3. Abu Salah

Perched on the edge of Hebron’s Old City, this busy restaurant is one of the best for shawarma. Shredded lamb or chicken wrapped up in pitta with hummous, garlic mayo and some pickles. The perfect lunch on a hot day. It has a handful of chairs where locals perch to chomp down on their pitas.

Landmarks


1. Dome of the Rock

Situated in the Old City’s Muslim Quarter, the Dome of the Rock marks the spot where the Prophet Mohammed ascended into heaven following the miraculous journey of one night from Mecca to Jerusalem, known as the Israa and Miraj. It is also the oldest and most exquisite Muslim shrine in the world. Built at the end of the seventh century by Umayyad Caliph Abdul Malik Ben Marwan, the mosque has a rectangular octagonal exterior and a specular gold-covered dome.


2. Nabi Musa

Nabi Musa is the Arabic name for the Prophet Moses, who is recognized as one of the most important prophets in Islam, as well as in Christianity and Judaism. Maqam an-Nabi Musa has been the site of an annual pilgrimage since the time of Salah ad-Din. Set in an awe-inspiring landscape 20 kilometers east of Jerusalem, the shrine is a splendid example of medieval Islamic architecture. The shrine, mosque, minaret, and some of the rooms at the site were built in 1269, and successive additions were added in 1475.


3. The Dead Sea

The Dead Sea, also known as the Salt Sea and the Sea of Lot, is a unique body of water in the Jordan Rift Valley. The Dead Sea is 85 kilometers long and 17 kilometers wide and covers an area of about 677 square kilometers. It lies about 417 meters below sea level, making the Dead Sea the lowest point on Earth. In addition, the Dead Sea is the world’s saltiest large water body, with a salt concentration ten times higher than that of the Mediterranean.
